# Inflammaging and Systemic Connection

## Overview

"Inflammaging" refers to the chronic, low-grade inflammation that characterizes biological aging [1]. The skin is both a major contributor to and target of inflammaging — its barrier function decline allows environmental triggers to penetrate, while senescent cells in all skin layers secrete pro-inflammatory factors (SASP) that propagate systemically [1]. This creates bidirectional connections between cutaneous health and whole-body inflammatory burden [1].

## Mechanisms of Skin Inflammaging

### Cellular Sources

**Senescent Cells:** Accumulate across all skin layers with age, secreting SASP factors including IL-1α, IL-6, TNF-α, and various chemokines [1]. These propagate senescence to neighboring cells via paracrine signaling ("secondary senescence") and can reach distant tissues through extracellular vesicles [1].

**Keratinocytes:** Act as stress sensors — upon UVR or pollutant exposure, release PGE2 (prostaglandin E2), cytokines, and increase ROS production [1]. Age-related decline in antioxidant defenses (α-tocopherol, glutathione, ascorbic acid) amplifies this response [1].

**Melanocytes:** More susceptible to oxidative lesions than keratinocytes upon UVA irradiation [1]. Senescent melanocytes drive skin aging via paracrine telomere dysfunction — they activate DNA damage responses in co-cultured dermal fibroblasts (Victorelli et al., EMBO J 2019) [1].

**Fibroblasts:** Dermal fibroblast senescence contributes to ECM degradation, reduced collagen production, and chronic inflammation [2]. The proteome of skin fibroblasts changes with age, affecting autophagy, ROS scavenging, and DNA repair pathways (Zhang et al., Aging Cell 2024) [2].

### Immune Evasion

Senescent cells evade immune clearance via HLA-E-mediated inhibition of NK and CD8+ T cell responses (Pereira et al., Nat Commun 2019) [1]. This allows persistent accumulation of pro-inflammatory senescent cells in aging skin [1].

## Systemic Connections

### Cardiovascular Disease [1]

- Transepidermal water loss rate is 23% higher in individuals with coronary artery disease (Solak et al., Skin Res Technol 2021) [1]
- Psoriasis and atopic dermatitis increase risk of heart attacks, strokes, type 2 diabetes, and obesity (Ascott et al., J Allergy Clin Immunol 2019; Holm & Thomsen, Psoriasis 2019) [4]
- Mechanism: Systemic inflammatory cytokines from skin → endothelial dysfunction → atherosclerosis [1]

### Bone Loss [1]

- Keratinocytes decrease cystatin-A secretion with age → reduced osteoblast/osteoclast progenitor proliferation (Liang et al., Nat Aging 2022) [1]
- Topical stimulation of cystatin-A production in mouse epidermis alleviated osteoporosis — demonstrating skin→bone signaling pathway [1]

### Cognitive Decline and Neurodegeneration [1]

- Atopic dermatitis and psoriasis increase risk of dementia and Alzheimer's disease (Yang & Man, Clin Interv Aging 2023) [1]
- Inflammaging implicated in neurodegenerative progression via systemic cytokine elevation [1]
- 3-year trial: topical emollient application prevented cognitive decline in elderly (>65 years, n=200) — treatment group showed stable cognitive scores vs declining controls (Ye et al., JEADV 2022) [1]

### Gut Inflammation (Skin→Gut Axis) [3]

- Skin inflammation activates intestinal stromal fibroblasts → promotes colitis (Dokoshi et al., J Clin Invest 2021) [3]
- Dermal injury disrupts gut microbiome and intestinal immune homeostasis in mice (Dokoshi et al., Nat Commun 2024) [3]
- Bidirectional: gut dysbiosis also causes cutaneous inflammation via "leaky gut" mechanism [3]

### Vitamin D3 Deficiency [1]

- Aged skin has reduced capacity to produce vitamin D3 from UVB exposure → systemic deficiency → altered physiology and increased cancer incidence (Bocheva et al., Int J Mol Sci 2021) [1]

## The Gut-Skin Axis [3]

A bidirectional, microbiota-driven relationship [3]:

**Gut→Skin:**
- Gut dysbiosis → increased intestinal permeability ("leaky gut") → microbial products/endotoxins enter bloodstream → cutaneous inflammation [3]
- Gut microbe-derived metabolites drive psoriatic inflammation via modulation of skin Th17 cells (Wang et al., Immunity 2025) [4]

**Skin→Gut:**
- Epidermal barrier dysfunction increases serum inflammatory cytokines (Hu et al., J Invest Dermatol 2017) [3]
- Skin wounding disrupts intestinal microbiome and immune homeostasis [3]

## Microbiome Dysbiosis in Aging [1]

- Skin microbiome composition changes over the human lifespan; can predict chronological age (Huang et al., mSystems 2020) [1]
- *S. aureus* overgrowth in atopic dermatitis replaces normally dominant *S. epidermidis* [1]
- Healthy skin microbiome promotes homeostasis, immune function, and antimicrobial peptide production [1]
- Skin microbiota serves as reservoir for antimicrobial resistance genes and nosocomial strains (Larson et al., Nat Aging 2022) [1]

## Therapeutic Implications [1]

**Anti-inflammatory strategies:**
- Exercise reduces circulating inflammatory factors and enhances dermal ECM (Nishikori et al., Sci Rep 2023) [1]
- Dietary micronutrients (lycopene, green tea catechins) protect against UVR-induced inflammation [1]
- Rapamycin inhibits IL-1α upregulation and SASP secretion in dermal fibroblasts [1]

**Microbiome-based therapies:**
- Fecal microbiota transplantation, dietary prebiotics, engineered bacteria supplementation (Sorbara & Pamer, Nat Rev Microbiol 2022) [3]
- Probiotic interventions for rosacea and atopic dermatitis (Sánchez-Pellicer et al., Front Microbiol 2024; Gao et al., Nutrients 2023) [3]
