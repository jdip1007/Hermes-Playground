# Hallmarks of Aging in Skin

## Overview

The 12 hallmarks of aging (López-Otín et al., Cell 2023) manifest distinctly in skin biology [1][2]. This concept maps each hallmark to its specific cutaneous expression, drawing from Furman et al.'s comprehensive 2025 review and related literature [1][2][3].

## The Four Categories of Hallmarks

### Primary Hallmarks (Direct Damage)

#### 1. Genomic Instability

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- UVR creates cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers (CPDs) and 6-4 photoproducts in DNA [1][2]
- Melanocytes more susceptible to oxidative lesions than keratinocytes upon UVA irradiation [3]
- Accumulation of DNA damage triggers premature melanocyte stem cell differentiation → hair graying [1][2]
- Hair follicle bulge stem cells resist rapid differentiation via Bcl-2 and accelerated DNA repair [1]

**Circadian dimension:** Hair follicle stem cells exhibit mitotic rhythmicity — slower growth in evening makes them time-of-day-sensitive to genotoxic stress. Epidermal clock gene *Bmal1* integrates central brain signals with peripheral rhythms for skin homeostasis (Mortimer et al., Cell Stem Cell 2024) [1].

**Field change:** High frequency of Notch driver mutations found in normal sun-exposed aged epidermis, creating clonal regions of preneoplastic epithelium that can give rise to multiple cutaneous squamous cell carcinomas (Martincorena et al., Science 2015) [1][2].

#### 2. Telomere Attrition

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- SOX2+ stem cell exhaustion → cellular senescence, premature aging phenotypes (kyphosis, hair graying, reduced fat mass) in mouse models [1][2]
- Shortened telomeres decrease stem cell mobilization, proliferative capacity, and hair growth [1]
- Telomerase re-expression (*Terc*) corrects dysfunctional epidermal hair follicle stem cells and attenuates chromosomal instability in keratinocytes (Siegl-Cachedenier et al., J Cell Biol 2007) [1][2]
- Epidermal deletion of DNMT1 → decreased hair follicle stem cell activation → progressive alopecia [1]

**Clinical relevance:** Telomere length correlates with visible aging signs — skin elasticity, thickness, and overall architecture. Telomerase-deficient mice show that telomerase activation can address skin aging without notably increasing carcinogenesis risk (though human UVR-exposed skin may differ) [1][2].

#### 3. Epigenetic Alterations

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- DNA methylation changes in hair follicle affect stem cell function and lineage plasticity [1][2]
- NAD+ levels modulate neural and melanocyte stem cell senescence via sirtuin activation (Zhang et al., Science 2016) [1]
- Epigenetic changes drive metabolic rewiring → mitochondrial dysfunction → hair follicle degeneration [1][3]
- CBP/p300 transcriptional coactivator is an evolutionarily conserved node promoting longevity in response to mitochondrial stress [1]

### Antagonist Hallmarks (Damaging Responses)

#### 4. Loss of Proteostasis

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- UVR damages ECM proteins directly; matrix metalloproteinases attempt removal but age-related proteostasis decline leads to protein damage accumulation [2][3]
- Skin fibroblast proteome changes with age affect autophagy, ROS scavenging, and DNA repair pathways (Tsitsipatis et al., Aging Cell 2022) [1][3]
- Oxidized and glycated proteins accumulate with impaired function [1][2]

#### 5. Mitochondrial Dysfunction

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- Decline in mitochondrial stress response (MSR) resilience is at the pinnacle of age-related decline at cellular, tissue, and organismal levels [1][3]
- Consequences: nuclear/mtDNA damage accumulation, deregulated metabolism (NAD+ depletion), epigenetic alterations, proteotoxicity, stem cell decline, senescence induction, chronic inflammation ("meta-inflammation") [1][2]
- NIX-dependent mitophagy eliminates damaged mitochondria during UVB-induced senescence (Cavinato et al., Aging Cell 2024) [1]

#### 6. Cellular Senescence

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- SASP factors propagate senescence via paracrine and juxtacrine mechanisms ("secondary senescence") [1][2]
- Extracellular vesicles from senescent cells carry SASP factors to distant tissues [3]
- Senescent melanocytes activate DNA damage response in co-cultured dermal fibroblasts (Victorelli et al., EMBO J 2019) [1][2]
- Dysfunctional telomeres of aged melanocytes upregulate HLA-E → inhibits NK/CD8+ T cell clearance of senescent cells (Pereira et al., Nat Commun 2019) [1]
- p16INK4A widely used as biomarker but not all senescence is p16-dependent [2][3]

### Integrative Hallmarks (Systemic Level)

#### 7. Stem Cell Exhaustion

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- Hair follicle stem cell activity declines with age; aged microenvironment dominates their properties [1][3]
- COL17A1 proteolysis → hair follicle miniaturization and hair loss (Matsumura et al., Science 2016) [1][2]
- Wnt signaling dysregulation affects self-renewal vs differentiation balance in bulge stem cells and interfollicular epidermal stem cells [1][3]
- Notch pathway changes affect hair follicle stem cell fate decisions [1]

#### 8. Disabled Autophagy (Macroautophagy)

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- Increased autophagosome formation in senescent keratinocytes and fibroblasts, but relationship is nuanced — both anti- and pro-senescence effects reported [2][3]
- Impaired clearance mechanisms rather than simple decline with age [1]
- mTORC1 aberrant signaling impairs autophagic activation; mTORC2 leads to chronic NF-κB activation [1][2]

#### 9. Deregulated Nutrient Sensing

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- IGF-1 down/mTOR up with age [1][2]
- Accumulation of advanced glycation end products (AGEs) with high glucose levels [2][3]
- Epidermis nutrition relies on vascularized dermis — aging compromises this supply chain [1]

#### 10. Altered Intercellular Communication

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- Diminished gap junction communication between skin cells [2][3]
- Deregulated cytokine signaling → chronic inflammation [1][3]
- Neuroendocrine and hormonal pathways change: skin acts as both target and source of neurotransmitters/hormones [1][2]
- Altered ECM-cell interactions disrupt fiber production [3]

#### 11. Chronic Inflammation (Inflammaging)

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- Keratinocytes act as stress sensors releasing PGE2, cytokines, ROS [1][3]
- Age-related decline in antioxidants (α-tocopherol, glutathione, ascorbic acid) amplifies environmental damage [2][3]
- Creates feedback loop: inflammation → ROS → DNA/protein/lipid damage → more inflammation [1][3]

#### 12. Dysbiosis

**Skin-specific manifestations:**
- Skin microbiome composition changes over lifespan; can predict chronological age (Huang et al., mSystems 2020) [1][3]
- *Staphylococcus aureus* overgrowth in atopic dermatitis (normally displaced by *S. epidermidis*) [3]
- Gut-skin axis: gut dysbiosis → increased intestinal permeability ("leaky gut") → microbial products enter bloodstream → cutaneous inflammation [1][3]
- Bidirectional: skin wounding disrupts gut microbiome and immune homeostasis (Dokoshi et al., Nat Commun 2024) [1]
