## Overview

*Bulie and Commissioner of Taxation (Taxation)* [2026] ARTA 1003 is a landmark Administrative Review Tribunal of Australia decision that overturned an ATO determination treating the applicant, Ragnar Bulie, as an Australian tax resident for FY2018–FY2022.[1] The tribunal found that under the Australia-Singapore tax treaty tie-breaker rules, Bulie should be treated as a tax resident of Singapore only — meaning his employment income was taxable exclusively by Singapore.[1]

## Facts

**Applicant:** Ragnar Bulie — dual tax resident of Australia and Singapore under domestic laws.[1]

**Physical presence during FY2018–FY2022:**[1]

- 173 days in Australia (FY2018)
- 78 days in Australia (FY2019)
- 189 days in Australia (FY2020)
- 144 days in Australia (FY2021)
- 108 days in Australia (FY2022)
- Overall: approximately 38% of time spent in Australia; ~62% in Singapore

**Housing:**[1]

- Jan 2018 – Aug 2022: leased residential property in Singapore
- Sept 2022 onward: another leased residential property in Singapore
- Owned a home in Sydney (non-income-producing during relevant period)

**Family:** Wife and youngest child continued living in the Sydney home since 2013.[1]

**Income:** Bulk earned from employment in Singapore during FY2018–FY2022.[1]

## Procedural History

1. ATO issued income tax assessments (May 2019 – Mar 2023) taxing Bulie's Singapore employment income as an Australian resident[1]
2. Bulie objected (Apr 2024), arguing treaty tie-breaker rules should treat him as a Singapore resident only[1]
3. ATO disallowed objection in full (Dec 2024)[1]
4. ARTA reviewed and overturned the ATO decision[1]

## Legal Framework: Treaty Tie-Breaker Rules

Since Bulie was a dual resident under both countries' domestic laws, the Australia-Singapore tax treaty tie-breaker hierarchy applied.[1] The tribunal needed to determine which country Bulie's personal and economic relations were closest to — the "centre of vital interests" test.[1]

The tie-breaker hierarchy:

1. **Permanent home** — available in both countries → inconclusive
2. **Centre of vital interests** (personal + economic relations) → decisive factor
3. Habitual abode — present in both countries
4. Nationality — not reached
5. Mutual agreement between competent authorities — not needed

## Tribunal's Findings

### Economic Relations — Singapore Wins Decisively

- Bulk of income earned from Singapore employment[1]
- Singapore characterised as *"centre of his day-to-day life and source of economic lifeline for himself and his family"*[1]
- Sydney home ownership dismissed: non-income-producing, did not alter *"actual, recurring economic activity which was centred in Singapore"*[1]
- Australian bank accounts, Medicare access, private health cover → *"administrative realities and conveniences of dual residency — does not have a significant bearing on the tie-breaker test"*[1]

### Personal Relations — Australia Stronger, But Not Enough

- Wife + youngest child in Sydney since 2013 (strong personal connection)[1]
- Bulie had *"meaningful personal relations in Singapore as well"*[1]
- Spent bulk of time (~62%) physically present in Singapore[1]

### Composite Finding

> *"During the Relevant Period, Mr Bulie spent the bulk of his time in Singapore and earned the bulk of his income in Singapore. Though his personal relations to Australia were stronger, he had meaningful personal relations in Singapore as well. And though his ownership of assets in Australia was not insignificant, they did not produce income and paled in their significance when compared with the primacy of his income-earning activity in Singapore."*[1]

## Significance

### Economic Centre of Gravity Overrides Family Location

The ATO had treated retained family ties (wife + child in Australia) as determinative. The tribunal explicitly acknowledged that personal relations to Australia were *stronger*, but economic relations tipped the balance anyway.[1] This establishes that you don't need your entire family with you overseas to claim non-Australian residency under treaty tie-breakers.

### Property Ownership ≠ Residency

Owning a home in Sydney didn't help Bulie's Australian residency case because it was non-income-producing.[1] The tribunal drew a sharp distinction between *asset ownership* and *active economic engagement*. This matters for countless dual residents who retain property but work overseas.

### Administrative Ties Dismissed as Irrelevant

Medicare access, private health insurance, Australian bank accounts — the ATO had been using these as evidence of Australian residency.[1] The tribunal called them *"administrative realities and conveniences"* with **no significant bearing** on the tie-breaker test.[1] This strips away common ATO arguments in dual-residency cases.

### Precedent for Split-Family Arrangements

Bulie's wife and youngest child stayed in Sydney while he worked in Singapore — a common arrangement among expats, dual-career couples, and families managing education/healthcare.[1] This ruling provides a clear pathway for the working spouse to claim non-Australian residency even when family remains behind. The ATO's own guidance acknowledges this complexity: *"The absence of his family doesn't mean that he'll never be regarded as residing here."*[2]

### Holistic Composite Test Affirmed Over Cherry-Picking

The decision reinforces that tie-breaker analysis requires weighing ALL factors together, not cherry-picking individual ones.[1] You can't win on family ties alone if economic reality points elsewhere. This is a check against the ATO's tendency to over-weight single factors (like family location) while downplaying others (like where income is earned).

## Practical Implications

- **For expats:** Working overseas while retaining Australian property/family ties doesn't automatically mean you're an Australian tax resident for treaty purposes[1]
- **For the ATO:** May need to reassess similar dual-residency cases where economic activity is clearly centred abroad[1]
- **For practitioners:** The "centre of vital interests" test now has clearer weighting — income-earning activity and time spent carry more weight than passive asset ownership or administrative ties[1]
- **For dual residents:** Lodge an early engagement advice request per ATO guidance before assessments are issued, rather than waiting for the ATO to make a determination that must then be appealed
