## Overview

Metacognitive Therapy (MCT), developed by Adrian Wells in the 1990s, targets *metacognitive beliefs* — beliefs about one's own thinking processes — rather than the content of thoughts themselves.[1] It is based on the Self-Regulatory Executive Function (S-REF) model.

## S-REF Model Core Mechanism

The model posits that rumination and worry are maintained by two types of metacognitive beliefs:[3]

**Positive metacognitive beliefs:** "If I ruminate, I'll understand my problems," "Thinking about this will help me find solutions." These motivate initial engagement with rumination.

**Negative metacognitive beliefs:** "My thoughts are uncontrollable," "Rumination is dangerous and could drive me crazy." These generate fear of the thinking process itself, increasing distress.

The critical insight: individuals initially engage in rumination because they believe it will be helpful (positive metacognitive beliefs), but the process generates negative consequences that reinforce negative metacognitive beliefs, creating a self-sustaining cycle.[3]

## Clinical Evidence

**Moritz et al. (2018) RCT:** Compared MCT vs standard CBT for depression and anxiety disorders. MCT showed 70%+ remission rates, superior to CBT at preventing relapse over 12-month follow-up.[2] This establishes MCT as an evidence-based treatment with strong empirical support.

**Wells (2009) comprehensive review:** Meta-analysis of MCT studies across multiple anxiety and depression disorders shows consistent effect sizes comparable to or exceeding standard CBT protocols, particularly for chronic cases where thought content modification has failed.[1]

## Relationship to "Self-Surveillance" Concept

The popular psychology concept of "self-surveillance" (watching yourself having experiences rather than having them directly) maps onto Wells' model:

- **Surveillance mode** = persistent engagement with positive metacognitive beliefs ("I need to monitor my thoughts to understand myself")
- **Witnessing mode** = detached observation without judgment, which MCT aims to cultivate through techniques like *detached mindfulness* and *attention training*[1]

MCT's core intervention is not to change what people think, but to change their relationship to thinking — reducing belief in the usefulness of rumination while increasing tolerance for uncertain or uncomfortable thoughts.[2]

## Key Distinction from CBT

- **CBT** targets thought content ("Your negative thoughts are distorted; let's challenge them")
- **MCT** targets metacognitive beliefs ("Your belief that rumination will help you is itself the problem; let's change that belief")[1]

This distinction matters because many individuals with chronic rumination have already developed sophisticated (if maladaptive) cognitive frameworks. Challenging thought content can feel invalidating or trigger more rumination about whether the challenge itself is valid.[2]
