# Auramine Orange Stain for Mycobacterial Detection in Cervical Lymphadenitis

> **Citation:** Cheng AG, Chang A, Farwell DG, Agoff SN. "Auramine Orange Stain With Fluorescence Microscopy is a Rapid and Sensitive Technique for the Detection of Cervical Lymphadenitis Due to Mycobacterial Infection Using Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology: A Case Series." *Otolaryngology–Head and Neck Surgery* 133(3): 381-385 (2005). DOI: [10.1016/j.otohns.2005.04.027](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.otohns.2005.04.027)
> **Authors:** Alan G. Cheng, Anthony Chang, D. Greg Farwell, S. Nicholas Agoff (University of Washington Medical Center, Seattle, WA)
> **Published:** September 2005

## Overview

This retrospective case series evaluates the effectiveness of auramine orange (AO) fluorescence staining for diagnosing mycobacterial cervical adenitis (MCA) from fine needle aspiration (FNA) cytology specimens. Among 19 patients evaluated at two Seattle urban hospitals (2000–2003), AO staining identified mycobacteria in **84.2% of cases** with results available within 4 hours — significantly faster than culture (2–4 weeks) and more sensitive than Ziehl-Neelsen (Z-N) staining.

## Key Findings

### Diagnostic Performance
| Test | Positive Results | Notes |
|------|-----------------|-------|
| **AO stain** | 16/19 (84.2%) | Results within 4 hours |
| Culture (Lowenstein-Jensen) | 12/19 (63.2%) | 9 TB, 3 NTM; results in weeks |
| AO-negative but culture-positive | 3/19 (15.8%) | All grew *M.* tuberculosis |

### Patient Demographics (n=19)
- **Gender**: 12 male, 7 female
- **Average age**: 30.6 years (range: 19–52)
- **Foreign-born or recent travel to endemic areas**: 84.2%
- **HIV-positive**: 3 patients
- **PPD-reactive**: 13 patients
- **Prior antimycobacterial therapy**: 5 patients

### Culture Discrepancy Analysis
- **4 AO-positive specimens** showed no culture growth — 3 of these 4 patients had received prior antimycobacterial treatment (explaining culture negativity)
- **3 culture-positive specimens** were AO-negative — none on prior therapy; likely low bacterial load below smear detection threshold
- Overall specificity: 75% (12/16 AO-positive confirmed by culture as TB or MAC)

### AO Stain Protocol
1. Stain slides with AO for 15 minutes → rinse with tap water
2. Decolorize with 0.5% HCl in 70% ethanol for 2 minutes → rinse
3. Counterstain with 0.5% potassium permanganate for 2 minutes → rinse
4. Fluorescence microscopy: Olympus BX40, ×200 scanning, ×500 confirmation

## Methodology

### Study Design
- Retrospective review of patients with clinical diagnosis of mycobacterial cervical lymphadenitis
- Two sites: University of Washington and Harborview Medical Centers, Seattle, WA
- Period: June 2000 – November 2003
- Initial enrollment: 23 patients; final analysis: 19 (4 excluded — 2 no records, 2 lost to follow-up)

### FNA Procedure
- Performed by experienced cytopathologist with 23-gauge needle
- Average of 3 aspirations per patient
- Air-dried smears processed for AO stain; corresponding cultures submitted on Lowenstein-Jensen media

### Species Identification
- GenProbe/Accuprobe DNA probes: *M.* tuberculosis complex and mycobacterium avium complex (MAC)
- TB complex positives subcultured on pyrazinamide agar to differentiate *M.* bovis from *M.* tuberculosis
- MAC not subclassified further

## Critical Analysis

### Strengths
1. **Rapid turnaround**: 4-hour result time vs. weeks for culture — critical for initiating treatment and infection control measures
2. **Detects both TB and NTM**: Unlike some molecular assays limited to specific species, AO stain detects all acid-fast bacilli
3. **Cost-effective**: Minimal reagent costs compared to PCR-based methods; accessible in resource-limited settings
4. **Complements culture**: Higher sensitivity than culture in patients on prior therapy (AO positive despite culture-negative)

### Limitations
1. **Small sample size (n=19)**: Case series design limits statistical power and generalizability
2. **No Z-N comparison**: The study doesn't directly compare AO to Ziehl-Neelsen staining in the same specimens — relies on literature for this comparison
3. **Retrospective design**: Selection bias possible; patients enrolled had clinical suspicion of MCA, not a broad screening population
4. **No species-level identification from AO stain**: Positive AO requires follow-up culture or molecular testing for definitive species ID and drug susceptibility
5. **Prior therapy confounder**: 5/19 patients on antimycobacterial drugs before FNA — this affects both culture sensitivity and potentially smear positivity
6. **Single-center experience**: Seattle urban hospitals may not represent global MCA epidemiology

### Clinical Relevance
- AO stain is superior to Z-N staining for mycobacterial detection (well-established in literature; this study confirms for cervical lymph node FNA specifically)
- Particularly valuable in settings where: (a) rapid diagnosis needed, (b) culture infrastructure limited, (c) PCR unavailable or too expensive
- Important adjunctive tool for otolaryngologists and pathologists managing neck masses with mycobacterial differential
- Does not replace culture (needed for drug susceptibility testing) but provides early presumptive diagnosis
