# Sustained Focus Strategies

A tactical framework for sustaining deep focus for 8+ hours daily, organized into four phases: entering focus, sustaining it, managing the final stretch, and the underlying life conditions required. Source: Lucas Duquette [1].

## Phase 1 — Entering Focus

### Entry Cost to Focus

When most people sit down to work, there's a **10–20 minute period** where focus is difficult. You feel agitated, work feels boring, something else is on your mind. After grinding through it, you finally lock in. This gap is the *entry cost* [1].

### Speed of Stimulation Mismatch

Root cause: working on deep tasks is **slow-stimulation** — things don't move quickly, no short-term gratification. Short-form content and social media are **fast-stimulation** — rapid dopamine hits, constant novelty. Switching from high-speed to low-speed activities forces the brain to readapt, creating the painful transition period [1].

### Bypassing Entry Cost: Immediate Work After Waking

Get to work as soon as possible after waking up. When you wake, your brain is naturally in a **low-stimulation state** — it's been offline for ~8 hours. The stimulation speed already matches what work requires, so there's no mismatch to overcome [1].

**What NOT to do before working:**
- No phone (obvious)
- No conversations with anyone — partner, parents, friends. Talking about exciting things stimulates you and makes the transition harder
- No sunlight exposure if focus is your priority — contradicts common health advice about morning light, but morning walks are stimulating. If focus > general health right now, skip them [1]
- Keep everything from waking to sitting at desk **very boring**

### Starting Rituals

A fixed sequence of steps followed every time before work. Programs the brain to expect focus as soon as these steps begin. More repetitions = stronger neural association → eventually triggers automatic focus mode [1].

**Example ritual:**
1. Write down the task on paper (what to accomplish in next hour)
2. Do five push-ups
3. Say an affirmation out loud
4. Smell a candle (olfactory cue — brain associates specific scent with work = instant trigger)
5. Play the exact same song every time

## Phase 2 — Sustaining Focus

### Work Sprints

A structured method to maintain full attention on one task for prolonged periods [1]:

**Step 1 — Clear Task:** Decide exactly what you'll accomplish in next 30, 45, or 60 minutes. Must be specific and quantifiable (e.g., "write a YouTube script", "make 30 cold calls", "send 50 DMs"). Vague goals don't work.

**Step 2 — Cut Time by 25%:** Estimate task duration, reduce by at least 25%, set timer. Rationale: we always overestimate (Parkinson's Law). Given more time → use all of it. Forced into tighter window → find ways to complete faster. Set a time that feels slightly too short — you'll still finish.

**Step 3 — Race Against Clock:** Creates stakes and pressure. Prevents tab-switching, phone-checking, talking to people. Urgency itself increases focus.

### Sterilized Environment (Subtraction > Addition)

Core principle: **focus is more about subtraction than addition**. Removing distractions works better than adding productivity tools [1].

**Protect Your Field of Vision:** Most distractions are visual — you see something, attention shifts, suddenly off-task.

- **Bland workspace:** Closed curtains (can't see outside), bare walls, minimal desk clutter. Deliberately boring environment
- **Work alone:** Tell housemates about focus mode so they don't interrupt every 10 minutes with questions
- **Browser tab discipline:** Only minimum tabs needed for current task. Not one more. When switching tasks → close everything, open fresh tabs for new task. Accumulated tabs = temptation when resistance hits

### Deep Rest (Not Phone Breaks)

Critical and counterintuitive. The only way to sustain 8-hour focus is through proper rest — but most people do breaks wrong [1].

**The mistake:** Taking a break → going on phone, scrolling social media, watching videos. This speeds up stimulation, making returning to work much harder (same entry cost problem from Phase 1).

**What to do instead:** Do nothing. Go outside, lie on bed, stare at ceiling, look out window. Must be **very boring** — not stimulating. Boring rest = deeply restful for brain → smooth return to work.

**When to take breaks:** Not on rigid timers (not "every 25 minutes"). Take a break when focus genuinely drops and isn't recovering. Minor resistance ≠ need for break — push through first. When focus is truly gone and not coming back, stop. Usually **5–10 minutes** of boring rest resets you.

### Post-Meal Recovery

After eating big meals → brain fog, focus disappears, long recovery time. Solution: take a **15-minute walk after meals**. Brain fog clears much faster than sitting still [1].

### Boredom Training (Lifestyle Level)

Get comfortable with boredom in everyday life as practice for work focus [1]:
- Waiting in line/doctor's office → don't immediately reach for phone when feeling tiny itch of boredom
- Watching movies → don't have phone open simultaneously
- Train to stay focused on one thing without expecting constant dopamine

Becomes a way of life. More comfortable with boredom outside work = easier to sustain focus during work when stimulation is naturally low.

## Phase 3 — The Final Stretch (Hours 6–8)

### Task Difficulty Matching

Key concept for lasting all day: **match task difficulty to remaining energy** [1].

Common failure pattern: start day with very difficult task, try to keep doing it for hours, hit hour 3–4 when energy is too low to sustain focus on that level → conclude "my focus is gone, I can't work anymore." Not true — energy just doesn't match task difficulty.

**Example schedule:**
- **Hours 1–4 (peak energy):** Hardest tasks — cold calling requires maximum mental effort and emotional resilience
- **Hours 5–7 (declining energy):** Second-tier tasks — lead sourcing for those calls. Still productive but less intense
- **Hours 7+ (low energy):** Learning activities — watch courses, take notes. Minimal creative output or decision-making required

Trying to maintain peak-difficulty all day is why most people burn out at 3–4 hours. Descend through difficulty levels → keep producing valuable work while matching natural energy curve [1].

**Action step:** Identify all tasks in your workflow, rank by effort required, schedule hardest first, progressively easier as day goes on.

## Phase 4 — The Hard Truth

### There Must Be Nothing Else to Do

Personal story from author: during pandemic, struggled to focus more than 2 hours/day on schoolwork because mind constantly pulled toward gaming, going out with friends, playing chess. Too many competing desires made sustained focus nearly impossible [1].

Core insight: **if you have too many hobbies, social plans, entertainment options in your life, sustaining focus on work will always be a battle.** Your brain has alternatives it wants to pursue.

Thought experiment: locked in room for a month with literally nothing else to do except work → you would focus. No other option. Focus wouldn't require effort — default state [1].

Conclusion: **simplify your life and make it pretty damn boring.** More effective than any productivity hack or step-by-step guide. Single most powerful thing for focus [1].

> "If you're still clinging onto the idea of balance, that is okay. This channel is not made for you."
>
> "But if your goals are right now the most important thing in your life, then this is what needs to happen."

Extreme focus requires temporary sacrifice of everything else. Not forever — but during periods when achieving specific goals matters most, cut out competing sources of stimulation and desire until work becomes the only meaningful option [1].
