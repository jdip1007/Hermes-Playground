## Overview

NAD+ precursor supplementation — primarily nicotinamide riboside (NR) and nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN) — has become one of the fastest-growing segments in the longevity supplement market, driven by animal studies showing that boosting NAD+ levels can extend lifespan and improve metabolic health.[1] However, recent findings that whole-blood NAD+ levels do not decline with age or respond to lifestyle interventions challenge some foundational assumptions underlying this approach.[1]

## The NAD+ Salvage Pathway

NAD+ cannot be directly supplemented due to poor cellular uptake. Instead, precursors enter the NAD+ salvage pathway:
- **Nicotinamide (NAM):** NAD+ breakdown product; recycled back to NAD+ via NAMPT (rate-limiting enzyme)
- **Nicotinic acid (NA):** Vitamin B3; enters via Preiss-Handler pathway
- **Nicotinamide riboside (NR):** Phosphorylated by NRK1/2 to NMN, then converted to NAD+ by NMNAT enzymes
- **Nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN):** Direct substrate for NMNAT enzymes; converted to NAD+[1]

## Key Supplements

### Nicotinamide Riboside (NR)

- Commercially available as NR chloride or NR hydrogen sulfate
- Patented formulations include Tru Niagen (ChromaDex, now part of Lonza)
- Claimed advantages: small molecule size allows cellular uptake; phosphorylated intracellularly to NMN then NAD+
- Human trials have shown increases in blood NAD+ levels after supplementation — but the Trętowicz et al. study suggests these acute elevations may not reflect sustained tissue-level changes[1]

### Nicotinamide Mononucleotide (NMN)

- Direct precursor to NAD+ via NMNAT enzymes
- Marketed by multiple supplement companies; some formulations claim enhanced bioavailability
- Controversy: FDA issued warnings in 2022 that NMN should not be marketed as a dietary supplement because it was first investigated as a drug[1]
- Human trials show mixed results on functional outcomes beyond blood NAD+ elevation

## Evidence Base

### Animal Studies (Supportive)

- Multiple mouse studies demonstrate that NR/NMN supplementation increases tissue NAD+ levels, improves mitochondrial function, and extends lifespan in specific genetic models
- Effects observed in muscle, liver, brain, and vascular tissues
- Most compelling data come from progeroid or genetically modified mice — translation to healthy human aging remains uncertain[1]

### Human Studies (Mixed)

- **Blood NAD+ elevation:** Multiple trials confirm that NR/NMN supplementation increases circulating NAD+ levels acutely[1]
- **Functional outcomes:** Evidence for improved insulin sensitivity, exercise performance, or cognitive function in humans is limited and inconsistent
- **Long-term safety:** Generally well-tolerated at doses up to 1000 mg/day, but long-term effects (>2 years) are not established
- **The Trętowicz challenge:** If whole-blood NAD+ is homeostatically regulated across lifespan, acute supplement-induced elevations may represent transient perturbations rather than meaningful biological changes[1]

## Market and Regulatory Landscape

### Market Size

- The global NAD+ precursor supplement market has grown rapidly since ~2018
- NR products (primarily Tru Niagen) have the most established commercial presence
- NMN market fragmented across multiple brands with varying quality control standards

### Regulatory Status

- **NR:** Generally recognized as safe (GRAS) status in some jurisdictions; sold as dietary supplement
- **NMN:** FDA issued warning letters (2022) stating NMN cannot be marketed as a dietary supplement because it entered clinical trials first — creating regulatory uncertainty[1]
- **Quality control:** Supplement industry self-regulated; independent testing reveals significant variability in actual NAD+ precursor content across brands

## Implications from Recent Findings

The Trętowicz et al. (2026) study raises several critical questions for the NAD+ supplementation field:[1]

### 1. Is Blood NAD+ a Meaningful Biomarker?

If whole-blood NAD+ is homeostatically maintained and doesn't vary with age, then using blood NAD+ as a surrogate endpoint in clinical trials may be misleading.[1] Elevated blood levels after supplementation may not correlate with tissue-level changes that drive functional outcomes.

### 2. Tissue-Specific vs. Systemic Effects

The study does not rule out tissue-specific NAD+ decline — it specifically addresses whole blood.[1] If aging-related NAD+ changes are compartmentalized (e.g., in neurons or muscle cells), then:
- Blood measurements are poor proxies for target tissues[1]
- Supplementation strategies may need to be tissue-targeted rather than systemic[1]

## Mechanism of Action Reassessment

If NAD+ precursors work through mechanisms beyond simply "boosting NAD+ levels" (e.g., modulating sirtuin activity, affecting NAMPT expression, or influencing circadian rhythms), then the focus on blood NAD+ as a primary readout may be misplaced.[1]

## NMN/NR Supplement Evidence from Literature

### Slc12a8 Transporter Controversy

NMN absorption in mice depends on the Slc12a8 transporter, which is absent in humans. This raises questions about whether oral NMN supplementation achieves meaningful intracellular concentrations in human tissues.[4] The absence of this transporter suggests that NMN may be degraded extracellularly by CD38 enzyme before reaching target cells.[4]

### Clinical Trial Results

- **Insulin sensitivity:** Some trials report modest improvements, but results are inconsistent across studies
- **Aerobic capacity:** Limited evidence for improved VO2 max or exercise performance in healthy adults
- **6-minute walk test:** Mixed results in older populations; some studies show no significant improvement over placebo
- **Blood NAD+ elevation:** Consistently demonstrated, but functional relevance remains unclear[4]

### CD38 Enzyme Degradation

CD38 is an NAD+-consuming enzyme expressed on immune cells and endothelial cells. It degrades extracellular NMN before it can reach target tissues.[4] This may explain why oral NMN supplementation shows limited efficacy in humans despite promising results in mouse models lacking CD38 expression patterns similar to humans.[4]

### Regulatory Status and Market Claims

- **NR:** Generally recognized as safe (GRAS) status in some jurisdictions; sold as dietary supplement
- **NMN:** FDA issued warning letters (2022) stating NMN cannot be marketed as a dietary supplement because it entered clinical trials first — creating regulatory uncertainty[1]
- **Quality control:** Supplement industry self-regulated; independent testing reveals significant variability in actual NAD+ precursor content across brands
- **High-cost unlicensed infusions:** In the UK, NAD+ infusions have been claimed to be "clinically proven" and "effective" treatment for alcoholism and drug abuse — but NAD+ is not approved or licensed for medical use[2]

## Open Questions

- Do specific tissues (brain, muscle, liver) show age-related NAD+ decline despite blood stability?[1]
- Can NAD+ precursors achieve meaningful intracellular concentration changes in target tissues at oral doses used in human trials?
- Are there subpopulations (e.g., metabolically unhealthy individuals) whose blood NAD+ regulation differs from the general population studied by Trętowicz et al.?[1]
- What is the optimal biomarker for assessing NAD+ precursor efficacy — blood levels, tissue biopsies, or functional readouts?
