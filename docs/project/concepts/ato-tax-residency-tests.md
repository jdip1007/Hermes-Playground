## Overview

The ATO applies four statutory tests to determine whether an individual is an Australian resident for income tax purposes.[1] The tests are applied in sequence — if the primary test (resides) is satisfied, no further tests are needed.[5] If not, each of the remaining three statutory tests is evaluated independently; satisfying any one makes the person an Australian resident.[5]

## Test 1: Resides Test (Primary)

The resides test is the primary test for working out if you're a resident of Australia for tax purposes.[2] The term "resides" is not defined within income tax legislation, so courts and the ATO rely on its ordinary meaning: *"to dwell permanently, or for a considerable time, to have one's settled or usual abode, to live, in or at a particular place."*[2]

### Factors Considered[2]

No single factor is decisive — all relevant facts and circumstances are weighed together:[2]

- **Period of physical presence** in Australia
- **Intention or purpose** of presence (stated intentions may differ from behaviour)
- **Family ties** — presence of family may indicate residency, but absence doesn't preclude it[2]
- **Business or employment ties**
- **Maintenance and location of assets** — dwelling (purchased or leased), motor vehicles, household effects[2]
- **Social and living arrangements** — local sports, community clubs, school enrolments, mail redirection[2]

### Key Principles

- Behaviour over time must show *"a degree of continuity, routine, habit"* to constitute residing[2]
- 6 months is considered a "considerable time" but is not a hard threshold — both time and behaviour matter together[2]
- Nationality is *"almost irrelevant"* except in borderline cases where all other facts are inconclusive[2]
- A person can be resident in more than one country simultaneously (dual residency)[2]

### Example: Family Absence

Yusef comes to Australia to work; his wife and 4 children remain in Iran. This is *"not generally consistent with the activities associated with residing"* but doesn't preclude residency — other factors like marital relationship state and behaviour while in Australia could still support a finding of residency.[2]

## Test 2: Domicile Test (First Statutory)

You're an Australian resident if your domicile is in Australia, **unless** the ATO is satisfied that your permanent place of abode is outside Australia.[3]

### What Is Domicile?[3]

Domicile is the place considered by law to be your permanent home — usually something more than a residence. You can only have one domicile at a time, whereas you may be resident in two or more places.[3] Three types exist:

- **Domicile by origin** — attributed at birth (nuptial child adopts father's domicile; ex-nuptial child adopts mother's)[3]
- **Domicile by choice** — inferred if there is both a change of residence and an intention to make the change permanent or indefinite[3]
- **Domicile by operation of law** — imposed by law (infant's domicile follows parents; married woman can acquire independent domicile; age of capacity fixed at 18 years)[3]

### Permanent Place of Abode

"Permanent" does not mean everlasting or forever, but is contrasted with temporary or transitory.[3] "Abode" means residence — where you live with your family and sleep at night.[3]

**Relevant factors for determining permanent place of abode:**[3]

- Intended and actual length of stay overseas, including continuity
- Existence of an established home overseas
- Existence of a residence in Australia (while overseas)
- Family and financial ties

### How the Test Works[3]

Two steps:

1. **Determine domicile** — if not in Australia, test is not satisfied; if in Australia, proceed to step 2
2. **Determine permanent place of abode** — if outside Australia, test is not satisfied; if in Australia, you're an Australian resident for income tax purposes

### Example: Foreign Resident Under Domicile Test[5]

Bronwyn (Australian resident) takes a 3-year overseas job with option to extend another 3 years. She, her husband and 3 children move overseas, rent out their Australian house, and establish a home abroad. Bronwyn is unsure if she'll extend. She is a **foreign resident** because: she fails the resides test (length of absence, circumstances inconsistent with residing in Australia), and under the domicile test, her permanent place of abode is outside Australia due to length of time overseas, establishment of a home overseas, family going with her, and renting out the Australian home.[5]

## Test 3: 183-Day Test (Second Statutory)

If you're in Australia for more than half the income year — continuously or intermittently — you will be a resident unless both:[4]

- Your *"usual place of abode"* is outside Australia, AND
- You have no intention to take up residence here

### Usual Place of Abode vs. Permanent Place of Abode[4]

These are distinct concepts:

| Term | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Usual place of abode** | The abode customarily or commonly used when physically in a country; must have attributes of a residence, not overnight/traveller accommodation | 183-day test |
| **Permanent place of abode** | Residence contrasted with temporary/transitory; where you live with family and sleep at night | Domicile test |

"Usual" means habitual, customary.[4] "Abode" means a dwelling or habitation — not transient accommodation.[4]

### Key Principles[4]

- Presence doesn't need to be continuous — all days physically present during the income year count (including arrival and departure)
- The test applies per **income year**, not calendar year
- If you've lived overseas most of your life, travel to Australia for 18 months in temporary accommodation/employment, then return overseas → usual abode remains overseas

### Example: Foreign Resident Under 183-Day Test[4]

Lars (Munich resident) gets a 12-month working holiday visa. He owns a home in Munich (not rented out), takes 12 months leave from work, arrives August 2024, has 5 different jobs while travelling around Australia, stays no place longer than 2 months, works only 7 of 12 months primarily to see the country. Although present >6 months in the year ended June 2025, he is **not** an Australian resident under the 183-day test because his usual place of abode remains outside Australia.[4]

## Test 4: Commonwealth Superannuation Test (Third Statutory)

This test applies to Australian government employees working at Australian posts overseas who are contributing members of the Commonwealth Superannuation Scheme (CSS) or Public Sector Superannuation Scheme (PSS).[5] It does not apply to members of the Public Sector Superannuation Accumulation Plan (PSSAP).[5] If applicable, you (and your spouse and children under 16) are an Australian resident regardless of any other factors.[5]

## Relationship Between Tests

The tests operate as a cascade:[5]

1. **Resides test** — if satisfied, stop; person is an Australian resident
2. **Domicile test** — if domicile in Australia AND permanent place of abode not outside Australia → resident
3. **183-day test** — if present >183 days AND usual place of abode not outside Australia → resident
4. **Superannuation test** — if CSS/PSS member at overseas post → resident

## Dual Residency and Treaty Tie-Breakers

When a person is found to be a dual resident under domestic laws, the relevant double tax agreement provides tie-breaker rules that determine which country has taxing rights.[2] The hierarchy typically proceeds: permanent home → centre of vital interests (personal + economic relations) → habitual abode → nationality → mutual agreement between competent authorities.
