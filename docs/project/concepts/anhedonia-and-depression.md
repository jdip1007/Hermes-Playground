## Overview

Anhedonia is a diverse array of deficits in hedonic function, including reduced motivation or ability to experience pleasure.[1] While originally defined as the inability to experience pleasure, modern research distinguishes between multiple facets: reduced anticipatory pleasure ("wanting"), reduced consummatory pleasure ("liking"), and deficits in reinforcement learning.[2]

## DSM-5 Core Symptom Status

The DSM-5 lists two core symptoms for Major Depressive Disorder diagnosis — only ONE is required:[3]

1. **Depressed mood** most of the day, nearly every day
2. **Markedly diminished interest or pleasure** in all, or almost all, activities (anhedonia)[3]

This establishes anhedonia as equally valid as sadness for depression diagnosis — not a secondary symptom but a primary criterion.[3]

## Types of Anhedonia

Kring & Eisner (1997) distinguish:[4]

**Consummatory anhedonia:** Inability to experience pleasure when engaged in rewarding activities. Associated with abnormalities in ventral striatum and medial prefrontal cortex during reward consumption.[2]

**Anticipatory anhedonia:** Inability to look forward to or be motivated by future rewards. More strongly linked to motivational deficits and associated with reduced activity in orbitofrontal cortex during reward anticipation.[4]

## Neural Mechanisms

Pizzagalli (2014) comprehensive review identifies key neural substrates:[2]

- **Ventral striatum hypoactivity** during reward anticipation correlates with anhedonia severity[2]
- **Prefrontal cortex dysfunction** — particularly orbitofrontal cortex involved in reward valuation[2]
- **Amygdala and anterior cingulate cortex abnormalities** linked to emotional blunting[2]

Critically, Pizzagalli shows anhedonia is one of the most robust predictors of treatment resistance in depression — patients with severe anhedonia respond less well to both pharmacological and psychotherapeutic interventions.[2]

## Depression as Exhaustion vs Sadness

The video's claim that "depression doesn't always look like sadness. Clinically, it often shows up as numbness" is clinically accurate:[3]

- Anhedonia presents as emotional flatness, not necessarily tearfulness[2]
- Many depressed patients report feeling "worn thin" rather than actively sad — consistent with the video's description of exhaustion-based depression[4]
- Life fatigue is a core but often overlooked symptom; many believe depression requires a grand cause (tragedy, loss) when chronic mental fatigue alone can be sufficient[2]
