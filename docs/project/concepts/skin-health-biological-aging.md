# Skin Health and Biological Aging

## Overview

The skin is the largest human organ and the most exposed to environmental stressors (the "exposome") [1]. A landmark 2025 review by Furman et al. in *Nature Aging* established that skin aging is not merely a cosmetic concern but a bidirectional process — skin health influences systemic biological aging, and systemic aging manifests through skin changes [1]. This positions the skin as both a biomarker window for organismal aging and a therapeutic target for extending healthspan [1].

## The Skin as an Aging Window

The skin's three-layer architecture (epidermis, dermis, hypodermis) makes it uniquely positioned to reflect cumulative environmental damage [1]:

- **Epidermis:** Keratinocyte barrier; thins with age, impairing waterproof function [1]
- **Dermis:** Fibroblasts, ECM (collagen/elastin), vasculature, nerves; loses density and elasticity [1]
- **Hypodermis:** Adipose/connective tissue; provides cushioning and insulation [1]

Visible signs of skin aging — wrinkles, laxity, pigmentation irregularities, impaired barrier recovery — are downstream manifestations of molecular hallmarks operating across all three layers [1].

## Bidirectional Skin–Systemic Connection

Skin degradation actively contributes to systemic aging through multiple pathways [1]:

| System | Mechanism | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Cardiovascular | Transepidermal water loss correlates with coronary artery disease severity; psoriasis/eczema increase heart attack/stroke risk | Solak et al. 2021; Ascott et al. 2019 |
| Bone | Keratinocyte cystatin-A secretion declines → reduced osteoblast/osteoclast progenitor proliferation → bone loss | Liang et al., Nat Aging 2022 |
| Brain/Cognition | Cutaneous inflammation linked to dementia risk; topical emollient prevented cognitive decline in elderly trial | Wen et al. 2022; Ye et al. 2022 |
| Gut | Skin wounding disrupts intestinal microbiome and immune homeostasis (skin→gut axis) | Dokoshi et al., Nat Commun 2024 |
| Immune | Reduced vitamin D3 production in aged skin → systemic deficiency → increased cancer incidence | Bocheva et al. 2021 |

## The Exposome Concept

Environmental exposures collectively accelerate biological aging [1]:
- **Ultraviolet radiation (UVR):** DNA photoproducts, ROS generation, melanocyte damage [1]
- **Pollution:** Particulate matter induces oxidative stress and inflammation [1]
- **Chemical disruptors:** Pesticides, plasticizers impair immune function [1]
- **Dietary factors:** Micronutrients (lycopene, green tea catechins) offer protection [1]

## Therapeutic Implications

The interconnectedness of skin and systemic aging suggests that maintaining skin health could positively affect whole-body aging [1]:

1. **Lifestyle:** Exercise reduces circulating inflammatory factors; sunscreen minimizes DNA damage [1]
2. **Senotherapeutics:** Senolytics (D+Q) clear senescent cells; senomorphics (rapamycin) suppress SASP [1]
3. **NAD+ boosters:** Nicotinamide riboside rescues melanocyte stem cell function and extends lifespan in mice [1]
4. **Mitochondrial medicines:** Urolithin A protects fibroblasts from photoaging via mitophagy induction [1]
5. **Topical interventions:** Emollient application, cystatin-A stimulation, antioxidant delivery [1]

## Current Gaps

- No consensus on clinical biomarkers for skin aging (p16INK4A widely used but insufficient alone) [1]
- Need multi-parametric approaches combining epigenetics, transcriptomics, metabolomics, proteomics [1]
- Animal models lack human complexity; organoids erase aging hallmarks via reprogramming [1]
- Microfluidics-enhanced 3D systems needed for multi-organ interaction modeling [1]
