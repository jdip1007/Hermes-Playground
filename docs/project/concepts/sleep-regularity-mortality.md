# Sleep Regularity as a Stronger Predictor of Mortality Risk than Sleep Duration

## Overview

A prospective cohort study using UK Biobank accelerometer data (n = 60,977) demonstrated that **sleep regularity** — the day-to-day consistency of sleep-wake timing — is a stronger predictor of all-cause and cause-specific mortality risk than sleep duration alone. Higher sleep regularity was associated with a **20–48% lower risk of all-cause mortality**.

## Citation

> Sleep Regularity as a Stronger Predictor of Mortality Risk than Sleep Duration: A Prospective Cohort Study. *UK Biobank accelerometer-based cohort study*. 60,977 participants aged 40–69 years, up to 7.8 years follow-up.

## Key Findings

### Sleep Regularity Index (SRI)
- SRI scores range from **0** (completely random sleep patterns) to **100** (perfect regularity)
- Measured objectively via **one-week accelerometer wear**
- Participants in the top SRI quintile had the lowest mortality risk compared to bottom quintile

### Mortality Risk Associations
- **All-cause mortality**: 20–48% lower risk with higher sleep regularity
- **Cardiometabolic mortality**: Significant predictor — irregular sleep linked to metabolic and cardiovascular disruption
- **Cancer mortality**: Irregular sleep patterns associated with increased cancer mortality, potentially via circadian disruption mechanisms
- **Other-cause mortality**: Also significantly predicted by sleep regularity

### Sleep Duration Comparison
- U-shaped relationship with mortality (both short and long durations linked to higher risk)
- Optimal duration: ~7–8 hours per night
- **Less predictive** of mortality than sleep regularity
- Short sleep associated with cardiometabolic mortality but not cancer mortality

## Mechanisms

### Circadian Rhythm Disruption
Irregular sleep patterns disrupt circadian rhythms, which regulate:
- Metabolic processes (glucose regulation, lipid metabolism)
- Cardiovascular function (blood pressure, heart rate variability)
- Inflammatory responses (cytokine production)
- Immune surveillance (relevant to cancer risk)

Regular sleep-wake timing stabilizes these physiological systems, promoting better overall functioning.

## Methodology Assessment

### Strengths
- **Large sample size** (60,977 participants) — one of the largest objective sleep studies
- **Objective measurement** via accelerometers rather than self-reported sleep diaries
- **Long follow-up period** (up to 7.8 years) for mortality tracking
- Comprehensive covariate adjustment (age, sex, ethnicity, physical activity, socioeconomic status, health conditions)

### Limitations
- **Single-week accelerometer data** may not capture long-term habitual sleep patterns — a one-week snapshot could be affected by temporary disruptions
- **Predominantly older adults of white ethnicity** from UK Biobank — limits generalizability to younger populations and non-white ethnic groups
- **Observational design** cannot establish causality between sleep regularity and mortality
- Potential for residual confounding despite covariate adjustment

## Clinical Relevance

### Public Health Implications
- Maintaining consistent sleep-wake schedules within a **1-hour window** may improve sleep regularity
- Interventions targeting sleep regularity may be more feasible than those focusing solely on extending sleep duration
- Public health guidelines should emphasize sleep regularity alongside sleep duration

### Actionable Recommendations
- Consistent bedtimes and wake times, even on weekends
- Regular meal timing to support circadian alignment
- Light exposure management (morning light, evening dimming)
