## Overview

Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+) has emerged as a molecule of interest in aging research due to its involvement in several hallmarks of aging and broad role in cellular metabolism[1]. Age-related declines in tissue NAD+ levels have been frequently reported, particularly in animal models, fueling the rationale for NAD+-boosting interventions using precursors such as nicotinamide riboside (NR), nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN), and their reduced counterparts (NRH, NMNH)[1].

A 2026 study by Trętowicz et al. at Amsterdam UMC rigorously challenged the assumption that blood NAD+ declines with age in humans[1]. Using a validated ultra-high-performance liquid chromatography coupled with high-resolution mass spectrometry (UHPLC-HRMS) method, they quantified whole-blood NAD+ across seven independent human cohorts totaling over 300 individuals[1]. The key finding: **whole-blood NAD+ levels remain remarkably stable with age and across lifestyle interventions**, but do change in response to NR supplementation as expected[1].

## Key Findings

### No Age-Related Decline in Blood NAD+

Across six cohorts examining age-related differences, no significant association was found between whole-blood NAD+ and age:

**Cross-sectional aging cohort** (n=40): Younger adults (<30 years) vs older adults (>60 years) showed no significant difference in NAD+ levels[1]. The study was powered to detect a ~7 nmol/ml difference with n=20 per group at 80% power[1].

**CardioHT cohort** (n=26): Age-stratified cohort (ages 28–73 years) showed no age association for NAD+ or other NAD+-related metabolites[1].

**ELITE cohort** (n=47 total): Elite athletes (n=23), younger non-athlete controls (<40 years, n=12), and older non-athlete controls (>40 years, n=12) — NAD+ levels did not differ between younger and older controls, and were similar in elite athletes vs younger controls[1].

**Leiden Longevity Study (LLS)** (n=70): Older individuals aged 63–87 years from long-lived families showed consistently stable NAD+ levels across this age range[1].

### No Lifestyle Intervention Effects

Two intervention trials in frail older adults showed no changes in whole-blood NAD+:

**TEAMS cohort** (n=65): 3-month intervention with exercise alone (n=31) or exercise plus protein-rich diet (n=34) — NAD+ levels remained unchanged in both groups[1]. Modest shifts were observed in some NAD+-related metabolites[1].

**MEJNES2019 cohort** (n=31): 6-month trial with control (n=7), nutritional supplementation (n=9), or multimodal exercise plus supplementation (n=15) — whole-blood NAD+ and related metabolites remained unchanged across all groups[1].

### NR Supplementation Increases Blood NAD+

**Twin-pair NR cohort** (n=24): Before and after 5 months of NR administration (escalated from 250 mg/day to 1 g/day), NAD+ levels increased as expected, confirming the dynamic range and sensitivity of the assay[1]. This positive control demonstrates that blood NAD+ CAN respond to interventions — it just doesn't change with age or lifestyle factors.

### Pre-Analytical Handling Is Critical

The study extensively characterized how sample handling affects NAD+ measurements:

- **Freezing causes significant loss**: Storing whole blood at -20°C for 4 days caused ~27% NAD+ loss; -80°C storage showed similar degradation[1]
- **Freeze-thaw cycles compound the problem**: Each cycle progressively reduced measured NAD+[1]
- **Small volume vs large volume**: Larger volume samples (1 ml) showed attenuated NAD+ loss compared to small aliquots (10 µl)[1]
- **Dried blood spots**: Showed degradation comparable to small-volume frozen samples[1]
- **Methanol preservation works**: Both freezing in methanol and thawing in methanol largely preserved NAD+ over 10 weeks of storage[1]

### Plasma vs Whole Blood

NAD+ concentrations in plasma were 50–100 times lower than in whole blood and fell below the limit of quantification (LOQ), supporting whole blood as the more robust matrix for NAD+ measurement[1]. This is consistent with NAD+'s intracellular localization.

### Method Validation

The UHPLC-HRMS method required only 10 µl human whole blood, using a stable-isotope-labelled internal standard (13C5-NAD+):

- **LOD**: 0.21 nmol/ml
- **LOQ**: 0.69 nmol/ml
- **Intra-assay CV**: 12.5%
- **Inter-assay CV**: 16.1%
- **Linearity**: R² = 0.9996 across tested range[1]

### Power Analysis Implications

The study performed power and sample size simulations showing that handling-related variability substantially increases sample size requirements for detecting small effect sizes[1]. Under observed variance structure, n=20 per group corresponds to a detectable difference of ~7 nmol/ml at 80% statistical power when accounting for one freeze-thaw cycle[1].

## Implications

### Challenge to NAD+ as Aging Biomarker

The results challenge the utility of blood NAD+ levels as a biomarker of aging or lifestyle factors[1]. Despite strong biological rationale from animal models showing tissue-specific NAD+ decline, human whole-blood measurements do not reflect age-related changes.

### Implications for NAD+ Supplementation Research

The fact that NR supplementation reliably increases blood NAD+ while natural aging does not decrease it suggests:
- Blood NAD+ may not be the best readout for assessing "NAD+ status" in aging[1]
- Tissue-specific measurements (e.g., muscle biopsies) may be more informative[1]
- The relationship between blood and tissue NAD+ pools is complex

### Methodological Recommendations

For future studies measuring blood NAD+:
- Use methanol-based preservation rather than conventional freezing[1]
- Account for freeze-thaw effects in study design and power calculations[1]
- Consider haematocrit as a potential confounder (though not significant in this study)[1]

## Limitations

- Cohort sizes were moderate (largest n=70), all cross-sectional or pre-post intervention designs limiting longitudinal assessment[1]
- Studies performed in three countries (Netherlands, Spain, Finland) but ethnicity was not recorded[1]
- Reduced NAD+-related metabolites (NADH, NADPH) could not be reliably semi-quantified across all cohorts[1]
- Haematocrit relationship with NAD+ was assessed informally — future studies should record haematocrit at blood collection[1]

## Related Research Context

Previous human studies on age-related NAD+ decline have been inconsistent: some reported age-associated declines, while others found no clear association in unadjusted analyses[1]. This study adds to the growing evidence that whole-blood NAD+ may not be a reliable aging biomarker in humans.

The niacin index (NAD+/NADP+) and niacin number (NAD+/(NAD++NADP+)) also showed no substantial changes across any cohorts, further supporting the stability of the broader NAD+ metabolome in blood[1].
