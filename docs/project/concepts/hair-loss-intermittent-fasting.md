## Overview

Intermittent fasting suppresses hair follicle regeneration through an interorgan signaling cascade involving adrenal glands, skin adipocytes, and hair follicle stem cells (HFSCs).[1] The mechanism is hormonal rather than metabolic sensing — mTORC1 nutrient sensing plays no role.[2]

## Mechanism: Adrenal → Lipolysis → HFSC Apoptosis

Fasting triggers a multi-step cascade:[1][2]

1. **Adrenal activation** — IF increases circulating cortisol and epinephrine[1]
2. **Skin lipolysis** — These hormones induce triglyceride breakdown in skin adipocytes near HFSC niches[1]
3. **Free fatty acid release** — Lipolysis releases FFAs into the HFSC microenvironment[1]
4. **HFSC apoptosis** — FFAs trigger oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and cell death in HFSCs[1]
5. **Hair cycle arrest** — Apoptotic HFSCs cannot initiate new hair growth cycles[1]

## IF Regimens Tested

Both tested regimens produced consistent hair growth suppression:[1]

- **16/8 time-restricted feeding** — 8h eating window, 16h fasting
- **Alternate-day fasting** — alternating fed/fasted days

## Rescue Strategy

Topical vitamin E application on fasted mice prevented HFSC apoptosis and restored hair growth to control levels by neutralizing oxidative stress/ROS.[1][2]

## Human Evidence

Clinical trial subjects undergoing IF showed elevated lipolytic hormones (cortisol, epinephrine), elevated fatty acids in skin, and halted hair growth — mirroring the murine mechanism.[1]

## Broader Context

### Stem Cell Context-Dependence

IF benefits some stem cells but harms others:[2]

| Stem Cell Type | IF Effect | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Hematopoietic | Promotes regeneration | Cheng et al. 2014[1] |
| Intestinal | Affects proliferation/tumorigenesis | Imada et al. 2024[1] |
| Pancreatic β-cells | Disrupts maturation | Matta et al. 2025[1] |
| Hair follicle (HFSC) | Triggers apoptosis → hair loss | Chen et al. 2025[1] |

### GLP-1 Agonist Connection

Telogen effluvium is a known side effect of GLP-1 agonist therapy — may share HFSC stress mechanisms with IF-induced weight loss.[1][2]

### Stress Hormone Paradox

Cortisol/epinephrine activate HFSCs during acute stress (hair darkening) but drive lipolysis → HFSC death during fasting — distinct pathways from the same hormones.[1][2]

## Significance

Dietary patterns can have organ-specific negative effects despite systemic metabolic benefits. Mice on IF showed positive metabolic health markers but still experienced hair growth disruption, suggesting stem cell responses to fasting are tissue-context dependent rather than uniformly beneficial.[1]

The adrenal → adipocyte → HFSC axis represents a novel interorgan communication pathway linking metabolism to dermatological outcomes.[2]
