## Overview

Australian tax residency determines whether an individual is liable to pay income tax on worldwide income in Australia or only Australian-sourced income.[1] The Australian Taxation Office (ATO) uses a separate framework from the Department of Home Affairs — immigration status and tax residency are not equivalent.[1] A person can be an Australian resident for tax purposes without being an Australian citizen or permanent resident, and may hold a visa to enter Australia while remaining a foreign resident for tax purposes.[1]

## Key Principles

**Australian residents for tax purposes** must declare all income earned in Australia and overseas.[1] They are taxed at resident rates with access to the full tax-free threshold.

**Foreign or temporary residents** are generally taxed only on Australian-sourced income, with different tax-free thresholds and withholding rules applying.[1]

## Residency Tests Framework

The ATO applies four statutory tests in sequence to determine residency status (see [Ato Tax Residency Tests](ato-tax-residency-tests.md)):[1]

- **Resides test** — the primary test; if satisfied, no further tests are needed
- **Domicile test** — applies when domicile is in Australia but person lives overseas
- **183-day test** — applies based on physical presence exceeding half the income year
- **Commonwealth superannuation test** — applies to Australian government employees at overseas posts

## Dual Tax Residency

A person can be a tax resident of more than one country simultaneously.[3] When dual residency exists, the relevant double tax agreement determines which country has taxing rights over certain classes of income to prevent double taxation.[3] The treaty "tie-breaker" provision shapes the tax liability — for example, Article 4.2 of the Australia/New Zealand tax treaty.[3]

For natural persons who consider themselves also resident in a treaty country, the ATO recommends lodging an early engagement advice request before assessments are issued.[3] For dual-resident non-individuals covered by treaties amended by the Multilateral Instrument (MLI), specific Competent Authority determination procedures apply.[3]

## Residency Status Changes During the Year

If status changes from resident to foreign resident during the income year, the person answers "yes" to "Are you an Australian resident?" on their tax return, ensuring they are taxed at resident rates with a pro-rata tax-free threshold for months as an Australian resident.[1] From the date of ceasing residency, foreign-source income no longer needs to be declared, and Australian-sourced interest, dividends, and royalties become subject to withholding tax provisions as final tax.[1]

## Common Situations

Per ATO guidance:[2]

- Leave Australia temporarily without setting up a permanent home elsewhere → generally an Australian resident
- Overseas student enrolled in a course longer than 6 months at an Australian institution → generally an Australian resident
- Visiting Australia, working and living in one location with steps to make it your home → generally an Australian resident
- Visiting Australia but travelling and working in various locations → generally a foreign resident
- Holidaying or visiting for less than 6 months → generally a foreign resident
- Migrating to Australia with intent to reside permanently → generally an Australian resident
- Leaving Australia permanently → treated as a foreign resident from the date of departure

## Certificate of Residency

A certificate of residency is a document showing, for a specific period, that the holder was an Australian resident for tax purposes, not a temporary resident, and liable to pay tax on worldwide income in Australia.[3] It may be needed by overseas tax authorities to confirm Australian residency status. Requested via form NAT 75441 (individuals) or NAT 75442 (non-individuals).[3]
