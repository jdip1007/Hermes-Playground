# Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR)

## Overview

AMR occurs when microorganisms evolve mechanisms to survive exposure to antimicrobial agents [1]. It is one of the top global health threats, with WHO estimating 1.27 million deaths directly attributable to AMR bacteremia in 2019 [1]. The rise of resistance threatens to undermine modern medicine's ability to treat common infections and perform complex procedures safely [2].

## Mechanisms of Resistance

- **Enzymatic degradation** — Beta-lactamases destroy penicillins/cephalosporins [1]
- **Target modification** — Altered PBPs, ribosomal mutations [1]
- **Efflux pumps** — Active export of antibiotics from cells [1]
- **Reduced permeability** — Porin mutations limiting drug entry [1]
- **Biofilm formation** — Physical barrier reducing antibiotic penetration [2]

## Priority Pathogens (WHO)

- **Critical**: Carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales, MRSA, VRE, drug-resistant Pseudomonas [1]
- **High**: ESBL-producing Enterobacterales, drug-resistant Neisseria gonorrhoeae, Salmonella [1]
- **Moderate**: Penicillin-resistant Streptococcus pneumoniae, drug-resistant Shigella [3]

## Detection Methods

- **Culture-based** — Gold standard but slow (48-72 hours) [1]
- **molecular** — PCR for resistance genes (bla_KPC, mecA, vanA) [1]
- **Whole-genome sequencing** — Comprehensive resistance gene profiling [1]
- **Rapid phenotypic** — Mass spectrometry, microfluidics-based assays [2]

## Surveillance

- **GLASS** — WHO Global Antimicrobial Resistance Surveillance System [1]
- **ECDC-ESGL** — European Surveillance of Antimicrobial Consumption [3]
- **Local** — Hospital antibiograms and infection control programs [2]

## Emerging Strategies

- Novel antibiotic classes (teixobactin, malacidin) [1]
- Phage therapy and phage-derived enzymes (endolysins) [1]
- Anti-virulence approaches (quorum sensing inhibitors) [2]
- Combination therapies and adjuvants [1]
- AI-driven antibiotic discovery [2]
