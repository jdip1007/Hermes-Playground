# Pandemic Influenza Emergence Mechanisms

**Related:** [Phylogenetics Phylodynamics](phylogenetics-phylodynamics.md), [Antimicrobial Resistance](antimicrobial-resistance.md), [Bioinformatics](bioinformatics.md)

## Overview

Pandemic influenza viruses emerge through **genetic reassortment** between previously circulating human strains and animal-origin virus segments, rather than direct introduction of a complete avian virus. This process occurs over years before pandemic recognition, primarily in mammalian intermediate hosts (especially swine). [1]

## 20th Century Pandemics Summary

### 1918 H1N1 Pandemic

- **Not** a direct avian-to-human jump — the BM/1918 virus was generated by reassortment between mammalian viruses and previously circulating human strains, likely in swine [1]
- Genetic components circulated in mammalian hosts (swine + humans) as early as 1911, at least 2–15 years before pandemic recognition [1]
- PB2 gene precursor present in mammals by ~1881 (BCI 1813–1912); M gene by ~1884 (BCI 1841–1911) [1]
- BM/1918 H1, N1, PB1, PA, NP genes clustered with human H1N1; PB2, M, NS genes clustered with swine lineages [1]
- **Seasonal H1N1 was NOT derived directly from BM/1918** — their precursors co-circulated during the pandemic (diverged ~1905) [1]
- Classic swine H1N1 is a reassortant between BM/1918 and an unknown virus, not a precursor of 1918 [1]

### 1957 H2N2 Pandemic

- Genetic reassortant: novel H2, N2, PB1 genes from Eurasian avian sources + remaining genes from circulating human strains [1]
- Introduced genes entered human populations 2–6 years before pandemic recognition (H2 HA TMRCA ~1955; NA ~1950) [1]

### 1968 H3N2 Pandemic

- Novel H3 and PB1 genes from avian sources + remaining genes from H2N2 virus [1]
- Introduction occurred between 1966–1968 (H3 HA TMRCA ~1963, PB1 ~1967) [1]
- H2N2 and H3N2 co-circulated for ~1–3 years in humans [1]

## Key Mechanisms

### Reassortment (Antigenic Shift)

Influenza A has 8 segmented RNA genes. When two different strains infect the same cell, gene segments can mix to produce novel reassortant viruses. This is distinct from point mutations (antigenic drift). [1]

- All three 20th-century pandemics involved reassortment between human and animal-origin segments [1]
- Multiple reassortment events over years, not single introduction events [1]
- Novel NA and internal genes may enter prevailing human strains BEFORE the novel pandemic HA is acquired [1]

### Swine as Mixing Vectors

Swine express both avian-type (α2,3-linked) and human-type (α2,6-linked) sialic acid receptors on respiratory epithelium, making them ideal mixing vessels for reassortment between avian and human influenza strains. [1]

- BM/1918 PB2, M, NS genes clustered with swine lineages [1]
- Frequent interspecies transmission between swine and humans supports swine as the likely site of reassortment before pandemic emergence [1]
- Lack of swine surveillance data from 1950s–60s means swine involvement in H2N2/H3N2 cannot be ruled out [1]

### Molecular Clock Dating (TMRCA)

Bayesian relaxed molecular clock methods (BEAST) estimate the **Time of Most Recent Common Ancestor** for each gene segment, providing timelines for interspecies transmission events. [1]

- Uncorrelated exponential clock model best fits influenza sequence data [1]
- SRD06 codon position model partitions first+second vs third codon positions [1]
- TMRCA estimates with 95% Bayesian credible intervals (BCI) provide time-bounds for transmission events [1]

## Surveillance Implications

Current global influenza surveillance focuses primarily on hemagglutinin (HA) for seasonal vaccine strain selection. Smith et al. argue this is **insufficient for early pandemic warning** because: [1]

- Novel NA and internal genes may enter human strains before the novel HA appears
- Precursor viruses with unremarkable HA antigens can carry pandemic potential in their internal gene segments
- High-throughput sequencing of all 8 gene segments is needed, especially from zoonotic hotspots (Asia) [1]

## The H1N1/1977 Re-emergence

All 8 genes directly derived from H1N1 viruses circulating in the 1950s. TMRCA estimates ~2–3 years before detection support accidental laboratory re-introduction rather than natural emergence. [1]

## Post-2009 Context

The 2009 swine-origin H1N1 pandemic (A/H1N1pdm09) validated Smith et al.'s framework: it was a triple-reassortant virus containing gene segments from North American swine, Eurasian swine, and avian lineages that had been circulating in pigs for years before spilling into humans. The virus contained reassortment products of human, avian, and classical swine H1N1 lineages — consistent with the multi-step reassortment model proposed here.

## Open Questions

- Exact zoonotic source(s) of 1918 pandemic gene segments remain ambiguous (avian vs mammalian intermediates)
- Role of swine in H2N2/H3N2 generation cannot be confirmed due to lack of historical swine sequence data from Asia
- Whether the multi-year precursor circulation pattern applies to future pandemics remains to be seen
