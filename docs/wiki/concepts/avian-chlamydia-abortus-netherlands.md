# Avian *Chlamydia abortus* Circulation and Community-Acquired Pneumonia Outbreak (Netherlands)

> **Citation:** Raven S, Heijne M, Koomen J, Doornenbal G, Maas M, Jacobs P, Keur I, Dijkstra F, Reukers D, Platenburg M, Verweij SP, Mager H-J, Totté J, Vainio S, Bongaerts M, Heddema E. "Circulation of avian *Chlamydia abortus* in the Netherlands and community-acquired pneumonia: an outbreak investigation and retrospective cohort study." *The Lancet Infectious Diseases* 25(2): 198-207 (2025). DOI: [10.1016/S1473-3099(24)00529-2](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1473-3099(24)00529-2)
> **Authors:** Stijn Raven*, Marloes Heijne, Jeroen Koomen et al. (Public Health Service Region Utrecht, Radboud University Medical Center, Wageningen Bioveterinary Research, National Institute for Public Health and the Environment [RIVM], Netherlands Food and Consumer Product Safety Authority, St Antonius Hospital, UMC Utrecht, Zuyderland Medical Center)
> **Published:** February 2025 | Online: October 16, 2024

## Overview

This study reports a family cluster of four patients with respiratory illness caused by avian *Chlamydia abortus* in the Netherlands (December 2022 – February 2023), providing the first convincing evidence of zoonotic potential for this newly classified pathogen. Three patients were hospitalized with community-acquired pneumonia (CAP), one requiring ICU admission and ECMO. Retrospective analysis identified 10 additional historical cases with the same *ompA* genotype between 2010–2022, all hospitalized, at least five with pneumonia, and one fatal. The study confirms both zoonotic transmission from wild birds and human-to-human spread.

## Key Findings

### Outbreak Cluster (4 Family Members)
| Patient | Severity | Hospitalization | Key Details |
|---------|----------|-----------------|-------------|
| Patient 1 | **Severe** — ICU + ECMO | Yes | CRP 418 mg/L; cleaned bird droppings with pressure washer 15 days before symptoms; dairy farm worker |
| Patient 2 | Moderate | No (outpatient) | CT 25.39 for *C.* psittaci PCR; same household/farm as Patient 1 |
| Patient 3 | Severe — CAP | Yes (general ward) | CRP 193 mg/L; relapse after initial doxycycline course; IgG titre 10× increase (1:100→1:1000) |
| Patient 4 | Moderate-severe — CAP | Yes (general ward) | CT 30.51 for *C.* psittaci PCR; relapse pattern; IgG titre 2.5× increase (1:400→1:1000) |

### Transmission Dynamics
- **Zoonotic source**: Wild bird feces — Patient 1 cleaned bird droppings from pasture pavement with shovel and pressure washer (aerosolization risk)
- **Human-to-human transmission**: Plausible for Patients 2, 3, and 4 (same household; temporal clustering; identical *ompA* genotype across all four)
- Environmental sampling of farm animals (cows, calves, cats) and bird droppings: mostly negative — source likely wild birds rather than livestock

### Retrospective Cohort (2010–2022)
- **10 historical cases** identified with the same aberrant *ompA* genotype (GenBank accession HE687292)
- All hospitalized; at least 5 developed pneumonia; **one death**
- Genotype initially thought to be most closely related to *C.* psittaci genotype C, but reclassified as avian *C.* abortus after 2021 taxonomic revision

### Molecular Characterization
- **Aberrant *ompA* genotype**: HE687292 — previously misattributed to *C.* psittaci genotype C
- Avian *C.* abortus strains distinguished from mammalian *C.* abortus by: (a) presence of a plasmid, (b) larger plasticity zone including cytotoxin gene — both associated with virulence
- Whole-genome sequencing performed on outbreak strain cultured from three patients
- XerC gene plasmid analysis used for further strain comparison

### Clinical Presentation
- **Symptoms**: Headache, fever, cough, dyspnoea, malaise — influenza-like progressing to pneumonia
- **Initial misdiagnosis**: All patients initially treated with doxycycline by general practitioners; Patients 3 and 4 deteriorated despite treatment (relapse pattern)
- **PCR detection**: *C.* psittaci PCR positive (cross-reactive assay detecting both *C.* psittaci and *C.* abortus); CT values 25–31
- **Serology**: Significant IgG titre increases; microimmunofluorescence cross-reactive among *Chlamydia* species

### Treatment Response
- Initial doxycycline (100 mg daily × 10 days) insufficient for some patients — relapse occurred ~3 weeks after initial treatment
- Hospitalized patients received: ceftriaxone IV + azithromycin oral, then switched to high-dose doxycycline (200 mg daily)
- Patient 1 required ICU-level care with ECMO and dexamethasone

## Methodology

### Outbreak Investigation
- Questionnaire interviews of all patients for exposure history (birds, animals, travel)
- Clinical data from medical records pre- and post-hospitalization
- Environmental sampling: bird droppings, farm animals (cows/calves nasal/vaginal swabs), cats (conjunctival swabs), corn feed

### Microbiological Diagnostics
- **PCR**: Real-time PCR targeting *ompA* or 23S rRNA gene — detects *C.* psittaci, *C.* abortus, *C.* caviae, and *C.* felis
- **Genotyping**: Partial *ompA* sequencing (variable domain IV) per National Human Psittacosis surveillance protocol
- **Serology**: Microimmunofluorescence (Euroimmun); positive thresholds: IgG >1:400, IgA >1:100, IgM >1:50; seroconversion or ≥4× IgG titre increase = recent infection
- **Culture**: Cell culture isolation from hospitalized patients with sufficient material

### Retrospective Analysis
- National Human Psittacosis (NHP) surveillance programme database (2010–2022)
- Strains with aberrant *ompA* genotype identified and compared via XerC gene plasmid analysis + GenBank comparison

## Critical Analysis

### Strengths
1. **First definitive evidence of zoonotic potential**: Avian *C.* abortus was classified in 2021 without known human pathogenicity — this study closes that gap conclusively
2. **Multi-disciplinary approach**: Combines clinical, epidemiological, microbiological, and genomic data with environmental investigation
3. **Retrospective component**: Identifying 10 historical cases reveals this is not an isolated incident but an ongoing public health concern
4. **Whole-genome sequencing**: Provides definitive strain characterization beyond *ompA* genotyping alone
5. **Published in high-impact journal** (*Lancet Infectious Diseases*) with accompanying commentary — significant visibility for public health action

### Limitations
1. **Small outbreak cluster (n=4)**: Limited statistical power; findings need confirmation from additional clusters
2. **PCR cross-reactivity**: The *C.* psittaci PCR detects multiple species — cannot distinguish avian *C.* abortus from *C.* psittaci at the point-of-care level without genotyping
3. **Environmental sampling largely negative**: Source attribution to wild birds is circumstantial (based on Patient 1's exposure history) rather than microbiologically confirmed from environmental samples
4. **Retrospective cases lack detailed clinical data**: Historical cases identified through *ompA* genotype alone; full clinical pictures unavailable
5. **Single-country study**: Netherlands-specific epidemiology may not generalize to other regions with different bird populations and surveillance systems

### Clinical/Public Health Relevance
- **Diagnostic gap**: Current PCR assays cannot differentiate avian *C.* abortus from *C.* psittaci — species-level identification requires post-PCR genotyping, delaying targeted public health response
- **Human-to-human transmission confirmed**: Infection control measures should include isolation of hospitalized patients and contact tracing
- **Severity spectrum**: From mild outpatient illness to ICU/ECMO-level disease — clinicians should consider *Chlamydia* spp in CAP differential even without bird exposure history (secondary cases had no direct animal contact)
- **Relapse pattern**: Initial doxycycline courses may be insufficient; longer or higher-dose regimens may be needed
- **Surveillance implication**: The Netherlands' National Human Psittacosis programme successfully identified this emerging pathogen — similar systems should be implemented elsewhere
